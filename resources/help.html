1. The \(\alpha \)-scale spaces

In the digital domain, smoothing leads to loss of resolution and, therefore, of some information. However, the information loss can be limited if one uses multiple smoothing scales Pauwels et al. [6] and later Duits et al. [2] introduced the \(\alpha \)-scale spaces for image processing. The basis of the approach is a generalization the heat equation. The resulting convolution kernels can be described best by the tools of fractional calculus. The kernel evolution is governed by two parameters – the scale s and the order of differentiation \(\alpha \). The approach leads to the fractional heat problem: \begin {flalign*} u\left (0, \mathbf {x} \right ) &= I\left ( \mathbf {x} \right ) \\ \partial _s u \left ( s, \mathbf {x} \right ) & = - (-\Delta )^{\alpha } u\left ( s, \mathbf {x} \right ) , \quad 0 \leq \alpha \leq 1 \end {flalign*}

where the Riesz fractional Laplacian operator is defined in the Fourier domain by: \[ (-\Delta )^{\alpha } U ( \mathbf {k} ) := k^{2\alpha } U ( \mathbf {k} ), \quad k= |\mathbf {k}|, \] where the \(k\) is the modulus of the wave vector \(\mathbf {k} \). Formally, the operator is extended by continuity for \(\alpha =1\) as \( (-\Delta )^{1} = \Delta \mapsto k^2 \), which corresponds to the usual Laplacian; and to identity for \(\alpha =0\), corresponding to invariance in the spatial domain.

2. Differential Invariants

There are several types of geometric features useful for image segmentation. Typical interesting image features are blobs, filaments and corners. The normal Laplacian \(\Delta _{ \perp G}\) presented below is sensitive to blobs, while its complement – the tangential Laplacian \(\Delta _{ || G}\) is sensitive to filaments.

Various differential invariants computed by the AS/IJ platform are presented in Table 1. Notable differential invariants are the amplitude \(|\nabla _{G} F|\) and the orientation of the gradient, the Hessian determinant, the Hessian eigenvalues, as well as the isophote \(\kappa \) and streamline curvatures \(\mu \) [3]. Up to a sign convention we have \begin {flalign} \kappa & = \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {n}^0 \\ \mu & = \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {\tau }, \quad \mathbf {\tau }= \mathbf {n}^{0} \cdot I_2 \end {flalign}

where \(I_2\) is the pseudoscalar of the Euclidean image plane. From the perspective of scale space theory the study of the differential invariants of the image reduce to the study of the differential invariants of the radially symmetric (generalized) heat kernel.

3. Orthogonal Laplacian Decomposition (LOD)

The simple representation of the gradient introduced above can be extended to a coordinate independent (!) operator using the tools of Geometric Algebra and Calculus. Readers are directed to [5] for an introductory material on the subject. The Laplace operator can be decomposed in two orthogonal components– on in the direction of the isophote and the other in the direction normal to the isophotes. The Laplacian of scalar function F can be decomposed into a normal component and tangential component, thus breaking the isotropy of the original operator [8]: \[ \nabla ^2 F = \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla \right )^2 F + \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla F\right ) \nabla \cdot \mathbf {n}^0 = \Delta _{\perp G}F + \Delta _{|| G} F \] where \(\mathbf {n} =\nabla F\) is the gradient, \(\mathbf {n}^0\) is the unit normal vector to the isophote curve \( F (x,y) =c\), \[ \quad \mathbf {n}^0 = {\mathbf {n} }/{|\mathbf {n} |} \] and \( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla \) denotes the directional derivative. Then also using the isophote curvature \begin {flalign} \Delta _{\perp G} &= \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla _G\right )^2 \\ \Delta _{|| G} &= \left ( \nabla _G \cdot \mathbf {n}^0\right ) \left ( \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla _G \right ) = \kappa \ \mathbf {n}^0 \cdot \nabla _G \end {flalign}

This is a coordinate-free definition of \(\Delta _{\perp }\) and \(\Delta _{|| }\), which can be specialized to any smooth coordinate system. This comes in contrast to the approach of ”gauge coordinates” employed in [3].

4. Gauss2D – Gaussian smoothing

The axiomatic linear scale space theory was formulated in series of works by Witkin and Koenderink [10, 4]. In its original version, the theory depends on several properties of the Gaussian filters as solutions of the diffusion equation in the scale-space generated by the image. That is, the generic smoothing kernel \(G\) is identified with a radially-symmetric Gaussian kernel of scale \(s=\sigma ^2 \in \mathbb {R}\) \[ G (r)= \frac {e^{-r^2/2s}}{2 \pi s } \] The Gaussian kernels provide several advantages: (i) they are rotationally invariant (ii) they do not produce artificial extrema in the resulting image (iii) successive convolutions with different kernels can be combined. Mathematically, this imposes a very useful semi-group structure, equivalent to the heat/diffusion equation. In this sense, the image structures diffuse or ”melt-down”, so that the rate of this diffusion indicates the ”robustness” of the structure.

6. Gaussian Derivatives

In the spatial domain, the Gaussian derivatives for the one dimensional case can be computed in closed form as \[ G_n \left (x \right ) = \frac {\partial ^n}{\partial x^n} G \left (x \right ) = \frac {(-1)^n} {\sqrt { 2 \pi s^{n+1}}} \,{He}_{n}\left ( {x}/{\sqrt {s}}\right ) \, e^{-\frac {x^2}{2s}} \] where \(He_n (x)\) is the statistician’s Hermite polynomial of order n. The sequence of statistician’s Hermite polynomials satisfies the recursion \[ He_{n+1} (x) =x He_{n} (x) - n He_{n-1} (x) \] starting from \(He_0 (x) = 1\) and \(He_1 (x) = x\). This allows for efficient simultaneous computation of all derivatives up to an order n in order to populate the the n-jet space. The n-jet components can be used to build the differential invariants up to order n.

7. Gaussian Structure – Structure tensor

The structure tensor (ST) is an abstract extension of the gradient. The tensor encodes the predominant directions of the gradient in a specified neighborhood of a point, and the degree to which those directions are coherent as a function of scale. Suppose that we have a scale-space representation of the gradient \(\nabla _G \). Then the structure tensor is the smoothed tensor product of the smoothed gradient vector[1]: \[ S_r (I) := G_r \star \{ \nabla _G I \cdot \nabla _G I^T \} \] From this expression it is apparent that the operator introduces smoothing on two scales. However, because of its quadratic characters the scales do not compose. \(S_r (I)\) can be represented by a 2x2 matrix.

8. LoG – Laplacian of Gaussian

The Laplacian operator can be thought of as the square of the gradient \(\nabla \). The analogy can be made precise using the tools of the Clifford algebra and Geometric Calculus: \[ \Delta = \nabla ^2 \] In Cartesian coordinates, the Laplacian has representation \[ \Delta _G= G_{xx} + G_{yy} \] where \( \Delta _{G}\) denotes the weak Laplacian operator. In a similar way, the Laplacian operator can be composed multiple times to give rise to the Power-of-Laplacian (PoL) operator [7]: \[ \Delta _G^n I = (\underbrace {\Delta _G \ldots }_{n \ \mathrm {times}}) I \] This operator enhances high-frequency features of an images given the scale cut-off. This can be seen easily from the frequency response of the LoG filter.

9. ALoG – Anisotropic decomposition of LoG

The theory of the anisotropic decomposition of LoG (ALoG) is given in Sec. . In Cartesian coordinates, the LoG is represented by \begin {flalign} \left ( G_x^2+ G_y^2 \right ) \Delta _{ \perp G} & = \left ( G_x^2 \right ) G_{xx} + \left ( 2 G_x G_y \right ) G_{xy} + \left ( G_y ^2\right ) G_{yy} \label {ed:lapo} \\ \left ( G_x^2+ G_y^2 \right ) \Delta _{|| G} &= \left ( G_x^2\right ) G_{xx} - \left ( 2 G_x G_y \right ) G_{xy} + \left ( G_y ^2\right ) G_{yy} \label {ed:lapt} \end {flalign}

10. Hessian

The weak Hessian tensor with respect to the kernel G is defined as the tensor product \[ \mathbb {H}_G (F) := \nabla _G \otimes \nabla _G F \] For smooth signals, the Hessian is symmetric and can be regarded as a metric tensor.

In Cartesian coordinates, this can be specialized to the usual formula \[ \mathbb {H}_G (F)= \left ( \begin {array}{ll} G_{xx} & G_{xy} \\ G_{xy} & G_{yy} \end {array} \right ) \star F \] where subscripts denote differentiation by coordinate variables. Furthermore, for the trace holds \[ \mathrm {Tr} \,\mathbb {H}_G (F) = \nabla _G^2 F = \Delta _{G} F \] where \( \Delta _{G}\) denotes the weak Laplacian operator. The eigenvalues \(\lambda _{1,2}\) can be determined locally from the equation \begin {equation} \mathrm {det} (\mathbb {H}_G (F)- \lambda \mathbb {I} )=0 \end {equation}

11. Curvature 2D

The planar image is represented as a set of isophote contours \[ F(x,y) = c \]

Isophote curvature \[ \kappa = \frac { G_{xx} G_{y}^2 - 2 G_{x} G_{y} G_{xy} + G_{x}^2 G_{yy}} {\left ( G_{x}^2 +G_{y}^2\right )^{3/2}} \]

Streamline curvature \[ \mu = \frac { G_x^2 G_{xy} - G_x G_y G_{xx} - G_{xy} G_y^2 +G_x G_y G_{yy} } {\left ( G_{x}^2 +G_{y}^2\right )^{3/2}} \]

12. Curvature 3D

The planar image is represented as a surface in the three-dimensional Euclidean space \( \mathbb {E}^3\), where the elevation represents the signal intensity. The image intensity generates a surface in 3D.

Mean curvature \[ \frac {1 }{2}\frac {\left ( 1+G_{x}^2\right ) G_{yy} - 2 G_{x} G_{y} G_{xy} +\left (1 +G_{y}^2 \right ) G_{xx} } {\left ( 1 + G_{x}^2 +G_{y}^2\right )^{3/2}} \]

Gaussian curvature \[ \frac {G_{xx} G_{yy} - G_{xy}^2} {\left ( 1 + G_{x}^2 +G_{y}^2\right )^2 } \]
